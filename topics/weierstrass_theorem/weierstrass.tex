\section{Weierstra√ü approximation theorem}
This section discuss the well-know theorem
\begin{theo}[Weierstra√ü]\label{theo|weierstrass}
	Let $f$ be a continuous function over a finite real interval $[a,b]$. For all $Œµ>0$, there exists a polynomial function $p$ over $[a, b]$ such that
	\begin{equation}
		\norm{f-p}_‚àû ‚©Ω Œµ
	\end{equation}

	In symbolic notation‚ÄØ:
	\begin{equation}
		‚àÄ [a, b]‚ààùïª(‚Ñù), ‚àÄ f‚àà\classC{0}{[a, b]}, ‚àÉ P‚àà‚Ñù[X], ‚àÄ Œµ>0, \norm*{f-\restr{\tilde{P}}{[a,b]}}_‚àû ‚©Ω Œµ
	\end{equation}
\end{theo}
This statement is what \cite{pinkus2000weierstrass} calls the \textquote{Fundamental Theorem of Approximation Theory}, slightly adapted to my taste. To my knowledge, it has actually never been stated in that form by Karl Weiertra√ü himself, who was more concerned by representing functions with power series, but it is an immediate consequence of \cite{weierestrass1885uber}. See \cite{pinkus2000weierstrass} more details of the history of that theorem.

In this section, we will often consider the trivially equivalent statement
\begin{theo}
	Let $f$ be a continuous function over a finite real interval $[a,b]$, then, there exists a sequence $\fami{f}{n}{‚Ñï}$ of polynomial functions that converges uniformly to $f$ over $[a, b]$.
\end{theo}

\subsection{Preliminaries}\label{sec|weierstrass|prelim}
Almost none of the classic proofs of \cref{theo|weierstrass} actually proves the full thing, but a special case for some fixed $a‚ÇÄ$ and $b‚ÇÄ$. This is not an obstacle, tough, since the following lemma proves that such a special case actually implies the whole theorem.

\begin{proof}
	Let
	\begin{equation}
		\fonct{h}{[a‚ÇÄ, b‚ÇÄ]}{[a, b]}{x}{\frac{b-a}{b‚ÇÄ-a‚ÇÄ}x+a-\frac{b-a}{b‚ÇÄ-a‚ÇÄ}a‚ÇÄ}
	\end{equation}
	then $h$ is an homeomorphism since non-constant and affine.

 	Let $f‚àà\classC{0}{[a, b]}$ and $Œµ>0$, then $f‚àòh‚àà\classC{0}{[a‚ÇÄ, b‚ÇÄ]}$ so there exists a polynomial function $p$  over $[a‚ÇÄ, b‚ÇÄ]$ such that for all $x‚àà[a‚ÇÄ, b‚ÇÄ]$,
	\begin{equation}
		\abs{f(h(x))-p(x)} ‚©Ω Œµ
	\end{equation}
	thus, for all $x‚àà[a, b]$,
	\begin{equation}
		\abs{f(x)-(p‚àòh‚Åª¬π)(x)} = \abs*{f\mleft(h\mleft(h‚Åª¬π(x)\mright)\mright)-p\mleft(h‚Åª¬π(x)\mright)} ‚©Ω Œµ
	\end{equation}
	and therefore
	\begin{equation}
		\norm*{p‚àòh‚Åª¬π}_‚àû ‚©Ω Œµ
	\end{equation}

	 To conclude, note that since $h$ is affine, $h‚Åª¬π$ is also affine, and thus $p‚àòh‚Åª¬π$ is polynomial.
\end{proof}

\subsection{–ë–µ—Ä–Ω—à—Ç–µÃÅ–π–Ω's probabilistic proof}

	\item It is a constructive proof.
	\item It introduces the Bernstein polynomial basis, which alo appears in other contexts e.g. in the construction of B√©zier curves.
	\item It uses some powerful probability theory results to avoid tedious algebraic manipulations, which makes the proof more concise and easily readable than most.
\end{itemize}

The following is essentially \cite{bernstein1913demonstration}, with more modern notations and less handwaving.
\begin{theo}[Bernstein Theorem]\label{theo|bernstein}
	Let $f‚àà\classC{0}{[0,1]}$ and for all $n‚àà‚Ñï^*$,
	\begin{equation}
		\fonct{f_n}{[0,1]}{‚Ñù}{x}{\Ex{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)}}
	\end{equation}
	where for all $(n,x)‚àà‚Ñï√ó[0,1]$, $X_{n,x}$ is a binomially distributed random variable with parameters $n$ and $x$.

	Then $\fami{f}{n}{‚Ñï^*}$ uniformly converges to $f$.
\end{theo}

This is relevant to the issue at hand, since
\begin{req}
	For all $x‚àà[0,1]$ and $n‚àà‚Ñï^*$,
	\begin{equation}
		f_n(x) = ‚àë_{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}x^k(1-x)^{n-k}f\mleft(\frac{k}{n}\mright)
	\end{equation}
	Thus, $f_n$ is polynomial, which proves \cref{theo|weierstrass}, according to \cref{sec|weierstrass|prelim}.
\end{req}

It is also not very surprising, the intuition of why $\fami{f}{n}{‚Ñï^*}$ should converge to $f$ can come from e.g. the weak law of large numbers‚ÄØ:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item For all $x$, $\frac{X_{n, x}}{n}$ converges in probability to $x$ when $n$ goes to $+‚àû$, since it is the empirical mean of $n$ independent Bernoulli random variables of mean $x$.
	\item Since it converges in probability, it also converges in distribution.
	\item Since it converges in distribution, and $f$ is a continuous, bounded (because continuous on a compact) function, $\Ex{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)}$ converges to $\Ex{f(x)}=f(x)$.
\end{enumerate}
This gives us the pointwise convergence. The uniform law of large numbers would also actually give us the whole proof, but it feels too much like cheating.

\subsubsection{The original proof}
Let us now state the original, probabilistic proof. It follows this outline
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Let $x‚àà[0, 1]$, we want an upper bound of the distance between $f_n(x)$ and $f(x)$ that does not depend on $x$ and will vanish as $n$ goes to $+‚àû$.
	\item To get it, since $f$ is uniformly continuous, we only need to control the distance between $\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}$ and its expected value $x$.
	\item To control that, we will use a classic probability theory technique‚ÄØ: we will split the universe between the event where the distance between $\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}$ and $x$ is smaller than some $Œµ>0$ (call it $A_{n, x}$), and the complentary event.
	\item Over $A_{n, x}$, we have what we want, and Bienaym√©-Chebyshev's inequality gives us an upper bound on $A_{n, x}^c$ that will not depend on $x$. So we have our control everywhere.
	\item Putting this together yields us what we want.
\end{enumerate}
\begin{proof}[Original proof of \nameref{theo|bernstein}]
	Let $Œµ>0$ and $x‚àà[0,1]$, for all $n‚àà‚Ñï^*$,
	\begin{align}
		\abs*{f_n(x)-f(x)}
			&= \abs*{\Ex{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)} - f(x)}\\
			&= \abs*{\Ex{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}}\\
			&‚©Ω \Ex{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}}	&& \text{Using the triangular inequality for integrals.}
	\end{align}

	Since $f$ is continuous over $[0,1]$, it follows from Heine-Cantor Theorem, that it is uniformly continuous. Thus, there exists $Œ¥>0$ such that for all $(x,y)‚àà[0,1]¬≤$, if $\abs{x-y}‚©ΩŒ¥$, then $\abs{f(x)-f(y)}‚©ΩŒµ$. Let then
	\begin{equation}
		A_{n,x}=\left\{\abs*{\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}-x}‚©ΩŒ¥\right\}
	\end{equation}
	we have
	\begin{align}
		\abs*{f_n(x)-f(x)}
			&‚©Ω \Ex{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}}\\
			&‚©Ω \Ex{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}ùüô_{A_{n,x}}} + \Ex{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}ùüô_{A_{n,x}^c}}
	\end{align}

	But over $A_{n,x}$, we have
	\begin{equation}
		\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)} ‚©Ω¬†Œµ
	\end{equation}
	And the following inequality holds everywhere, so it is holds on $A_{n,x}^c$
	\begin{equation}
		\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)} ‚©Ω 2\norm{f}_‚àû
	\end{equation}

	Thus
	\begin{align}
		\abs*{f_n(x)-f(x)}
			&‚©Ω\Ex{\underbrace{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}}_{‚©ΩŒµ}ùüô_{A_{n,x}}}
				+ \Ex{\underbrace{\abs*{f\mleft(\frac{X_{n,x}}{n}\mright)-f(x)}}_{‚©Ω2\norm{f}_‚àû}ùüô_{A_{n,x}^c}}\\
			&‚©Ω Œµ\Ex{ùüô_{A_{n,x}}} + 2\norm{f}_‚àû \Ex{ùüô_{A_{n,x}^c}}\\
			&‚©Ω Œµ\underbrace{P(A_{n,x})}_{‚©Ω1} + 2\norm{f}_‚àû P\mleft(A_{n,x}^c\mright)\\
			&‚©Ω Œµ + 2\norm{f}_‚àû P\mleft(A_{n,x}^c\mright)\label{eqline|bernstein1}
	\end{align}

	But, since $\Ex{X}=nx$ et $\Var(X)=nx(1-x)$, it follows from Bienaym√©-Chebyshev's inequality \parencite{chebyshev1867valeurs} that
	\begin{align}
		P\mleft(A_{n,x}^c\mright)
			&= P\mleft(\abs*{\frac{X}{n}-x}>Œ¥\mright)\\
			&‚©Ω \frac{\Var\mleft(\frac{X}{n}\mright)}{Œ¥¬≤}\\
			&‚©Ω \frac{x(1-x)}{nŒ¥¬≤}\\
			&‚©Ω \frac{1}{nŒ¥¬≤}\label{eqline|bernstein2}
	\end{align}


	Finally, injecting \refeq{eqline|bernstein2} into \refeq{eqline|bernstein1} get us
	\begin{align}
		\abs*{f_n(x)-f(x)}
			&‚©Ω Œµ + 2\norm{f}_‚àû P\mleft(A_{n,x}^c\mright)\\
			&‚©Ω Œµ + 2\norm{f}_‚àû \frac{1}{nŒ¥¬≤}\\
	\end{align}
	for all $x‚àà[0,1]$, and thus, since $Œ¥$ is independent of $x$,
	\begin{align}
		\norm{f_n-f}_‚àû
			&‚©Ω Œµ + 2\norm{f}_‚àû \frac{1}{nŒ¥¬≤}\\
			&\xrightarrow[n]{} Œµ\label{eqline|bernstein3}
	\end{align}

	Therefore, since \refeq{eqline|bernstein3} holds for all $Œµ>0$,
	\begin{equation}
		\norm{f_n-f}_‚àû ‚ü∂ 0
	\end{equation}
	i.e.
	\begin{equation}
		f_n \xrightarrow[\text{unif.}]{} f
	\end{equation}
\end{proof}
